Tourism and Hospitality

Definitions 1

Tourism is the act of traveling for pleasure or business. Tourists can visit different places, such as cities, countries, or natural attractions. They can also participate in different activities, such as sightseeing, shopping, or dining out.

Hospitality is the act of welcoming and providing services for guests. In the context of tourism, hospitality refers to the services that are provided to tourists, such as accommodation, food and beverage, and entertainment.

The tourism and hospitality industries are closely related. The tourism industry provides the demand for hospitality services, while the hospitality industry provides the supply of these services.

Here are some examples of tourism and hospitality industries:

  • Accommodation: This includes hotels, resorts, bed and breakfasts, and other places where tourists can stay.

  • Food and beverage: This includes restaurants, bars, cafes, and other places where tourists can eat and drink.

  • Entertainment: This includes theme parks, museums, theaters, and other places where tourists can be entertained.

  • Transportation: This includes airlines, buses, trains, and other ways that tourists can get around.



Definitions 2

Tourism: Tourism is when people travel to different places for fun, leisure, or to learn about new cultures. Tourists visit famous landmarks, beautiful nature spots, historical places, and exciting cities. They want to experience different types of food and experience local traditions. Tourism is a way to explore the world, meet new people, and create wonderful memories.

Hospitality: Hospitality is about being kind and welcoming to guests and travelers. Hospitality providers, such as hotels and restaurants, try to give you a comfortable and enjoyable experience. They provide you with a place to stay, delicious food to eat, and friendly service. Hospitality is about making sure that visitors have a pleasant and enjoyable experience during their trip.

Examples:

  • Tourism: Imagine you visit a famous city like Paris and see the Eiffel Tower, try delicious French pastries, and take a boat tour on the Seine River. That's tourism!

  • Hospitality: When you stay in a hotel, the staff greets you with a smile, gives you a clean room, and helps you with any questions you have. That's hospitality!


The Typical Tour –Sightseeing

The typical tour is based on a person’s travel route and the cities and places where they plan to stop or stay. The tour includes visiting famous landmarks and popular sites. Tourists will also enjoy fine dining and shopping excursions. The typical plan is: Eat, Shop, and see famous places.


Themed/Specialty Tours

Themed or specialty tours are a great way to explore a specific interest or topic while traveling. They cater to a wide range of interests and preferences, providing participants with a unique and immersive experience. Here are some examples of themed tours:

  • Historical Tours: These tours focus on exploring historical sites, landmarks, and cities. They often include visits to museums, ancient ruins, and famous battlegrounds, allowing participants to learn about the past and the events that shaped it.

  • Culinary Tours: Food enthusiasts can enjoy culinary tours that showcase the local cuisine and culinary traditions of a particular region or country. Participants may visit local markets, take cooking classes, and dine at renowned restaurants to experience the flavors of the destination fully.

  • Wine Tours: For wine connoisseurs, wine tours take them to famous vineyards and wineries. These tours often include wine tastings, pairing sessions, and discussions with winemakers to learn about the wine-making process.

  • Adventure Tours: Adventure-themed tours cater to adrenaline junkies and outdoor enthusiasts. Activities like hiking, rock climbing, white-water rafting, zip-lining, and safaris are included, offering an exhilarating and action-packed experience.

  • Literary Tours: Ideal for bookworms, these tours take participants to locations featured in famous books or the hometowns of renowned authors. The tours often include literary discussions and book readings.

  • Photography Tours: Photography enthusiasts can join these tours to capture stunning landscapes, wildlife, and cultural moments. Professional photographers often lead these tours, providing tips and guidance to improve photography skills.

  • Wellness Retreats: These tours are designed to promote relaxation, mindfulness, and well-being. Activities might include yoga sessions, meditation, spa treatments, and healthy cooking classes.

  • Music and Festival Tours: These tours center around music and festivals, taking participants to iconic music venues, concerts, and cultural festivals worldwide.

  • Art Tours: Ideal for art lovers, these tours explore museums and art galleries. They usually center on both modern and classic art works.

  • Haunted and Ghost Tours: For those who love a spooky adventure, haunted and ghost tours take participants to alleged haunted locations such as castles, hotels, and houses.

  • Architecture and Design Tours: These tours delve into the world of architecture and design, exploring iconic buildings, modern landmarks, and the work of famous architects.

  • Wildlife and Nature Tours: Nature enthusiasts can join wildlife tours to observe and learn about animals in their natural habitats, such as safaris, birdwatching tours, or whale-watching expeditions.

  • Sports Tours: Sports-themed tours can include attending major sporting events, visiting sports museums, and participating in sports-related activities.

  • Fashion and Shopping Tours: For fashionistas, these tours take participants to fashion capitals, boutique shopping districts, and exclusive fashion shows.

Event Planner/Manager/Coordinator

An events manager in the hospitality industry is a professional responsible for planning, organizing, and executing various types of events and functions within hotels, resorts, conference centers, or other hospitality establishments. They play a crucial role in ensuring that events run smoothly and successfully, providing memorable experiences for guests and clients. Here are some key aspects of their role and responsibilities:

Types of events:

  1. Corporate Conferences: Organizing large-scale conferences for businesses, including arranging keynote speakers, breakout sessions, workshops, and networking opportunities.

  2. Weddings: Coordinating all aspects of weddings, from venue selection and decor to catering, entertainment, and managing the wedding day itself.

  3. Trade Shows and Exhibitions: Planning and managing trade shows or exhibitions where companies can showcase their products and services to potential clients and partners.

  4. Charity Galas and Fundraisers: Handling events aimed at raising funds and awareness for charitable organizations, including auctions, dinners, and entertainment.

  5. Business Meetings and Seminars: Managing smaller-scale business meetings, seminars, workshops, or training sessions for corporate clients.

  6. Social Events: Planning private celebrations such as birthday parties, anniversary parties, engagement parties, and other special occasions.

  7. Award Ceremonies: Coordinating ceremonies to recognize and honor individuals or organizations for their achievements and contributions.

  8. Team-Building Events: Organizing team-building activities and retreats for companies or organizations to enhance team cohesion and communication.

  9. Educational Conferences: Managing conferences focused on specific industries or academic fields, involving presentations, panel discussions, and networking opportunities.

  10. Fashion Shows: Coordinating events to showcase the latest fashion collections of designers or fashion brands.

Duties:

  1. Event Planning: Events managers are involved in the initial planning stages of an event. They collaborate with clients or internal stakeholders to understand their requirements, objectives, and budget constraints. Based on this information, they develop a comprehensive event plan that outlines the logistics, timeline, and resources needed.

  2. Venue Selection: One of the crucial tasks for an events manager is to identify and secure suitable venues for various events. They evaluate different spaces within the establishment, considering factors such as capacity, layout, and amenities, and overall ambiance to match the event's theme and requirements.

  3. Vendor Coordination: Events managers work closely with external vendors and suppliers, such as caterers, decorators, audiovisual technicians, and entertainment services. They negotiate contracts and ensure that vendors deliver accordingly.

  4. Budget Management: Managing budgets is a critical aspect of an events manager's role. They need to allocate resources efficiently, track expenses, and find cost-effective solutions without compromising the quality of the event.

  5. Event Marketing and Promotion: Depending on the event's nature, events managers may be involved in marketing and promoting the event to attract attendees or guests. This could involve coordinating with the marketing team to create promotional materials, managing social media campaigns, and reaching out to potential participants.

  6. Logistics and Operations: Events managers oversee all logistical aspects of an event, including setting up and arranging the venue, managing registrations, coordinating transportation, and ensuring all required equipment and materials are in place.

  7. On-site Management: During the event, the events manager takes charge of on-site operations. They supervise the event staff, manage last-minute changes or adjustments, and handle any emergencies that may occur.

  8. Post-Event Evaluation: After the event, events managers conduct post-event evaluations to assess the success of the event, gather feedback from clients and attendees, and identify areas for improvement in future events.


Tour Guide


Duties of a Tour Guide:


  • Conducting tours: Lead groups of tourists through various attractions, landmarks, and points of interest while providing informative commentary and answering questions.

  • Planning itineraries: Designing well-organized tour schedules so tourists know when and what to expect.

  • Providing historical and cultural context: Sharing knowledge about the history, culture, and significance of the places visited.

  • Ensuring safety: Prioritizing the safety of the tourists during the tour, especially in potentially hazardous locations.

  • Managing logistics: Organizing transportation, accommodation, and dining arrangements for the tour group.

  • Interacting with tourists: Building rapport with tourists, engaging with them, and addressing their needs and concerns.

  • Handling unexpected situations: Being adaptable and resourceful when dealing with unforeseen circumstances during the tour.

  • Dealing with emergencies: Knowing how to respond to medical incidents, lost individuals, or other emergencies. Must have first Aid, CPR, and AED training.

  • Promoting local businesses: Recommending and supporting local restaurants, shops, and services.


Skills of a Tour Guide:


  • Communication: Excellent verbal communication skills to deliver engaging and informative commentary clearly to tourists of different backgrounds and ages.

  • Public speaking: Confidence in speaking to large groups and maintaining their attention throughout the tour.

  • Interpersonal skills: Being friendly, approachable, and able to connect with people from diverse cultures.

  • Multilingual ability: Proficiency in multiple languages can be a valuable asset in catering to international tourists.

  • Time management: Efficiently managing time to stick to the tour schedule without feeling rushed.

  • Problem-solving: Quick thinking and creative problem-solving to handle unexpected challenges during the tour.

  • Cultural knowledge: A deep understanding of local customs, traditions, and etiquette to provide cultural context.

  • Customer service: A focus on providing exceptional customer service to ensure tourists have a positive experience.

  • Flexibility: Adaptability to changing circumstances or weather conditions.

  • Leadership: Confidence and assertiveness to lead and guide the group effectively.

  • Storytelling: The ability to tell captivating stories and anecdotes to make the tour more engaging.

  • Empathy: Understanding and addressing the needs and preferences of different tourists.

  • First aid and CPR training: The ability to administer basic first aid CPR in case of medical emergencies during the tour.

Employee engagement

Employee engagement pays off by reducing turnover, increasing productivity, and increasing revenue. Ultimately, engaged employees lead to restaurant success!

Employee engagement reduces turnover


The annual venue employee turnover rate stands at a staggering 73%. Engaged employees tend to be happier employees, and are therefore less likely to leave your venue for another employer. According to Cornell’s Center for Hospitality Research, turnover costs the hospitality industry $5,864 per lost employee. Think about what retaining one, five, or 10 employees over a year through engagement could mean for your business.

Engaged employees are more productive


In addition to the thousands of dollars associated with hiring and training new employees, turnover also costs venues productivity. Even before disengaged employees quit, they’re less motivated by their work so they don’t perform at their maximum potential and are more likely to take sick days. Engaged employees, on the other hand, take 10 times fewer sick days than disengaged employees.

Engaged employees produce more revenue


Did you know that highly engaged employees bring in 147% more profits than their less passionate peers? When front-of-house staff are inspired by their work, they can upsell and serve more authentically, which makes customers more receptive to their offers.


Engaged employees work harder, make customers happier, and generate more revenue for your venue than disengaged employees. When you know how to engage employees at your venue, you’ll build a solid team that will lead your venue to success.



How to engage employees at your venue


1. Reinforce your venue's mission, vision, and values


Share your venue's mission, vision, and/or values during employee onboarding. When employees understand that they’re working towards a larger goal, they’ll feel more passionate about their work.


Offer recognition with things like employee of the month or performance bonuses and rewards to team members who embody your venue's values.


2. Create team bonding activities


A recent study of the restaurant industry by 7shifts found that three-quarters of restaurant employees say the best part of working in the industry is their colleagues. When employees feel like they’re part of a family, they’ll be more engaged and will look forward to working with their colleagues. In fact, work friendships increase employee satisfaction by 50%.Create a sense of community among your team by making team meals a tradition, hosting social events for employees outside of work, or organizing holiday parties.


3. Celebrate employee milestones and personal dates


Let employees know that you care about them by acknowledging employment anniversaries, birthdays, and other meaningful personal occasions. Giving your team members shout outs on social media, singing happy birthday during a team meeting, or acknowledging an employee’s child’s birthday in your team’s communication app will mean a lot.


Showing employees that you care about them beyond their professional capacity goes a long way in making employees feel more engaged at work.


4. Gamify performance


A little friendly competition can go a long way to boost sales, morale, and employee engagement. Offer incentives to your team for specific goals like upselling, decreasing ticket times, or selling a special menu item.


Be creative with prizes to make the competition worthwhile. You could offer the winner a paid day off from work or concert tickets, for example. Gamifying performance is just one creative way to engage employees.


5. Create a mentorship program


Pair new employees with more experienced staff members to serve as mentors throughout their employment. Tell employees to think of their mentors as someone between a manager and a friend - someone they can go to for advice on how to adjust to the venue's idiosyncrasies, and someone they can celebrate victories with.


Providing new team members with mentors shows them that you and the venue's management team care about their professional development and well-being, which will ultimately increase engagement and reduce turnover.


6. Provide professional development opportunities


Invest in your employees’ success and they’ll be more invested in the success of your venue. Set aside a budget for professional training opportunities for your team. You can set individual budgets or team budget that managers can spend on group professional development.


Typsy’s online video courses are a convenient and budget-friendly way to help employees grow in the hospitality industry.


7. Make it easy for team members to stay in touch


Facilitating communication among your team members will help them work together better by fostering a feeling of kinship. A good employee scheduling tool will also have a team communication component, enabling employees to communicate with their team members while managing their shifts from their phones.


When employees can easily communicate with each other, both on the clock and outside of work, they’ll be more committed to their work.


Employee engagement is the passion that employees have for their work that compels them to put discretionary effort into what they do. Engagement increases productivity, grows your revenue, and reduces turnover. When you go the extra mile and invest in your employees by making them feel connected and acknowledged, they’ll invest in your venue through their work ethic and loyalty.
