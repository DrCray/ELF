Simply Sentences

A straightforward guide to English sentence structure — with only a few rules to remember.

Welcome

Grammar is complicated, especially when you are first learning it. Simply Sentences is designed to make things as easy to understand as possible. The focus is on sentence structure.

The rules and explanations are useful, but they are not as important as the example sentences. Our brains are great at recognizing patterns — even when we don't fully understand the "why" behind them. Reading these sentences again and again will help you see the patterns of basic English sentence structure.

The key idea

In this approach, we don't start by looking for the subject and verb. Anyone who has seen a long sentence knows how difficult that can be. Instead, the first step is to identify everything that cannot be the subject or verb. Once we've done that, what's left is the subject and verb — the heart of the sentence.

Think of it like finding coins hidden under a big pile of feathers. You can't see the coins directly — so you gently fan the feathers away until they're all gone. What remains are the coins. In our sentences, the feathers are the extra details that add meaning, and the coins are the subject and verb (along with any objects).

The Building Blocks — In a Nutshell

Here are all the pieces we'll be working with across these lessons. Don't worry about memorizing them right now — you'll see each one in detail on its own page.

Nouns

A noun is a word for a person, place, thing, idea, or quality.

car, dog, justice, beach, laptop, happiness

Adjectives

Adjectives describe or modify nouns.

big car  ·  large black book  ·  happy little puppy

Prepositions

Prepositions show relationships — usually of place, time, or direction.

at the store  ·  under the table  ·  behind the chair  ·  with my mother

Subjects

The subject is who or what the sentence is about.

Mark baked a cake.

Verbs

The verb expresses an action or state.

Mark baked a cake.

Adverbs

Adverbs tell more about the verb — how, when, where, or to what degree.

Mark eagerly baked a cake.

Adverbs sitting right next to the verb are grouped with it as a single unit: eagerly baked.

Direct Objects

The direct object receives the action of the verb — it answers what? or whom?

Mark eagerly baked a cake.

Indirect Objects

The indirect object is the person or thing that benefits — it answers for whom? or to whom?

Mark eagerly baked Mary a cake.

Verbals

Verbals are verb forms that are not the main verb. They form phrases that add extra information.

Mark eagerly baked a cake to give to Mary.
Singing his favorite song, Mark eagerly baked Mary a cake.
Mark enjoys baking cakes for Mary.

These elements are at the heart of every sentence. You don't need to know all of grammar to be proficient in English — but these basics cannot be ignored.
