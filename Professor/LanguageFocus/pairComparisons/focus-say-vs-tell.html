📰 Overview

Learn the difference between say and tell — one of the most common grammatical errors made by English learners at every level. The distinction is simple: say focuses on the words or message, while tell requires a listener directly after it. This lesson covers grammar patterns, reported speech, and practical examples to help you use each verb correctly.

🎯 The Core Contrast

SAY = focus on the words / message — no listener required  |  TELL = focus on the listener — always needs a person after it

SAY 🗣️ focus on the words · no object needed
Focus is on the words or message expressed. No listener is required in the sentence — you can say something without naming who heard it.
📌 say + something | say + (that) clause | say + to + person
📖 Example: "She said she would be late — but she didn't explain why, and now the whole team is waiting."
📖 Example: "The manager said that the project deadline had been moved to Friday, which gave everyone only two extra days."
📖 Example: "What did he say? I was on the phone and missed the beginning of the announcement."
TELL 📢 focus on the listener · always needs a person
Focus is on the person receiving the information. A listener (object) is always required directly after "tell" — you tell someone something.
📌 tell + someone + something | tell + someone + (that) | tell + someone + to (verb)
📖 Example: "He told his manager that he was resigning — it came as a surprise because he had never complained about the job before."
📖 Example: "She told the team to submit all files by Thursday, but three people still hadn't sent anything by Friday morning."
📖 Example: "Why didn't you tell me there was a problem? I could have helped you if I had known earlier."
🎯 Usage Guidelines practical map + examples
💡 Note: These are guidelines to get you started, not strict rules.
🗣️ Use SAY when...
the focus is on the message or words — you don't need to name who heard it. Use say to if you include the listener.

📘 "She said the meeting was cancelled — but nobody got a notification."

📘 "He said to me that he wasn't interested, but his body language told a completely different story."

📢 Use TELL when...
the focus is on the person receiving the information. Always include the listener directly after "tell."

📘 "She told me the meeting was cancelled — I was relieved because I hadn't prepared yet."

📘 "Can you tell the team that Friday's deadline has been extended? I'll send a formal email later."

✨ Test: "She ___ she was tired." (no listener named → said)  |  "She ___ me she was tired." (listener named → told)
